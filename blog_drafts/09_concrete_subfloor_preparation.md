# DIY vs. Pro: How to Prepare Your Concrete Subfloor for Hybrid Flooring

The beauty of a finished floor is entirely dependent on what lies beneath it. 

One of the most common—and catastrophic—mistakes made during home renovations in Victoria is ignoring the condition of the subfloor. Homeowners often assume that because a concrete slab *looks* flat, it is ready for new flooring. Unfortunately, this is rarely the case. 

Modern hard flooring, particularly rigid SPC Hybrid Flooring, requires an incredibly flat and stable surface. If you install premium planks over a wavy, uneven concrete slab, the floor will eventually bounce, creak, and the locking joints will crack under pressure. 

In this technical guide from Portland Flooring, we explain exactly why subfloor preparation is the most critical step of any installation, how to check your concrete slab, and whether leveling is a DIY job or best left to the professionals.

## The Golden Rule: Flat vs. Level

First, we must clarify a crucial industry distinction: your floor does not necessarily need to be perfectly *level* (parallel to the horizon), but it absolutely must be perfectly *flat* (smooth, with no dips or humps). 

If a house is built on a slight, consistent slope, hybrid flooring can be installed perfectly. However, if there are localized hills and valleys across the concrete slab, the floor will fail. 

**The Tolerance Standard:** Most hybrid flooring manufacturers dictate that the subfloor must be flat to within **3mm over a 2-meter radius**. Anything outside of this tolerance must be repaired before installation begins.

## How to Test Your Concrete Subfloor

Before purchasing flooring, you must evaluate your slab. 

1. **Clean the Slab:** Sweep and vacuum the bare concrete to remove all dust, old glue, and debris. 
2. **The Straightedge Test:** Purchase a long (2-meter or 3-meter) aluminum straightedge or a very long spirit level.
3. **Scan the Room:** Lay the straightedge flat on the concrete. Get down on your hands and knees and look for gaps between the bottom of the straightedge and the concrete. 
4. **Measure the Gaps:** If you find a dip, slide a tape measure or coins under the gap. If the gap is deeper than 3mm, you have a "low spot." If the straightedge rocks back and forth like a seesaw, you are resting on a "high spot."

## The Dangers of Skipping Preparation

If you lay a rigid hybrid plank over a 5mm dip in the concrete, what happens? 

When you walk over that specific area, your body weight forces the rigid plank to bend down into the dip. This flexing puts immense strain on the delicate "click-lock" mechanism that holds the planks together. Over a few months of continuous flexing, the plastic lock will snap. The planks will separate, creating visible gaps that trap dirt and destroy the waterproof integrity of the floor. This completely voids the manufacturer's warranty.

## Fixing High Spots: Concrete Grinding

If your slab has localized humps or ridges (often found where two concrete pours meet), they must be removed. 
- **The Process:** This requires a heavy-duty industrial concrete grinder equipped with diamond blades. The machine aggressively grinds the hump down until it is flush with the surrounding floor.
- **The Danger:** Grinding concrete produces a massive amount of highly toxic silica dust. Professional grinders use industrial HEPA vacuums attached directly to the machine to capture the dust. 

## Fixing Low Spots: Self-Leveling Compound

If your slab has dips, craters, or a rough texture, it must be filled.
- **The Process:** A liquid "self-leveling compound" (a specialized, fast-setting cement) is mixed and poured over the low spots. Gravity pulls the liquid flat, filling in the valleys before it sets rock-hard.
- **The Danger:** Mixing the compound requires precise water ratios. If mixed incorrectly, it will dry too soft and crumble under the flooring. The concrete must also be primed with a specialized chemical beforehand, otherwise, the leveling compound will not adhere and will peel off the slab.

## DIY or Professional?

Can you prepare a concrete subfloor yourself? 
- If you only have one or two tiny dips that require a trowel of patching compound, a competent DIYer can handle it.
- However, if the entire room is wavy, requires heavy grinding, or involves pouring large areas of self-leveling compound, **we strongly advise hiring a professional.**

Flooring installers possess the heavy machinery, industrial vacuums, and the trained eye required to make a slab perfectly flat. The cost of paying a professional to level your floor is microscopic compared to the cost of replacing a brand-new hybrid floor that has cracked due to a bad DIY prep job.
